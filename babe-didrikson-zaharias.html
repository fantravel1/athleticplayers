The Girl Who Refused to Be Ladylike

Mildred Ella Didrikson was born on June 26, 1911, in Port Arthur, Texas, to Norwegian immigrant parents. She was the sixth of seven children in a working-class family that struggled to make ends meet.

Her father, Ole, was a ship's carpenter. Her mother, Hannah, took in washing to help support the family. Money was always tight. Childhood was hard. But the Didrikson kids were tough, competitive, and fearless.

Young Mildred‚Äînicknamed "Babe" after Babe Ruth because of her prodigious home-run hitting in neighborhood baseball games‚Äîwas a tomboy in an era when girls were expected to be delicate and demure.

She climbed trees. She played baseball, basketball, and football with the boys. She ran faster, jumped higher, and threw farther than anyone in her neighborhood‚Äîboys included.

Her mother worried. "Why can't you act like a lady?" she would ask. Babe's response: "Because I'm not one."

The world in the 1920s and 30s had rigid ideas about what women could and couldn't do. Women weren't supposed to be athletic. They weren't supposed to compete. They weren't supposed to sweat, grunt, or show strength. Those things were "unfeminine."

Babe didn't care. She knew she was the best athlete anyone had ever seen. And she wasn't going to apologize for it.

In high school, she dominated every sport she tried. She led her basketball team to the state championship. She set records in track and field. She played organized baseball. She even excelled at swimming, diving, and tennis‚Äîsports she barely practiced.

After high school, she got a job with the Employers Casualty Insurance Company in Dallas, which sponsored a women's basketball team and track team. Babe joined both. And she dominated both.

The Price of Being Great

After the 1932 Olympics, Babe Didrikson should have been celebrated as a national hero. Instead, she faced a wave of criticism, mockery, and sexism that nearly destroyed her career.

Sportswriters called her "mannish" and "unfeminine." They questioned her gender. They suggested she wasn't a "real woman" because of her athletic abilities and muscular build.

One columnist wrote: "It would be much better if she and her ilk stayed at home, got themselves prettied up and waited for the phone to ring."

The Amateur Athletic Union, looking for any excuse to punish her for challenging gender norms, stripped her of her amateur status in 1933 after she appeared in a car advertisement. The punishment was absurd and vindictive‚Äîmale athletes appeared in ads all the time without consequences.

Without amateur status, Babe couldn't compete in most track and field events. At 22, her Olympic career was over.

So she reinvented herself. She barnstormed across the country, playing exhibition basketball games, baseball games, and billiards matches. She pitched in minor league baseball games‚Äîstriking out major leaguers. She played in golf exhibitions. She even toured with a vaudeville act, playing the harmonica and joking with audiences.

She did whatever it took to make money and stay in the public eye. And through it all, she kept practicing golf‚Äîa sport that didn't have the same amateur restrictions as track and field.

Conquering a New Sport

In 1935, Babe decided to become the greatest golfer in the world. She had dabbled in golf before, but now she approached it with the same obsessive dedication she had brought to track and field.

She practiced 12-16 hours a day. She hit golf balls until her hands bled. She studied every aspect of the game‚Äîswing mechanics, course strategy, mental toughness. She worked with the best coaches. She played thousands of rounds.

And within a few years, she became the best female golfer in the world.

In 1946 and 1947, Babe won 17 consecutive golf tournaments‚Äîa record that has never been matched. She won 10 major championships, including three U.S. Women's Opens. She revolutionized women's golf with her power and athleticism.

Before Babe, women's golf was a genteel, country-club sport for wealthy amateurs. Babe brought power, competitiveness, and professionalism. She hit the ball farther than anyone. She played aggressively. She transformed the sport.

In 1950, she helped co-found the Ladies Professional Golf Association (LPGA), creating opportunities for women to compete professionally and earn a living from the sport.

Babe became a household name. She appeared on magazine covers. She won the Associated Press Female Athlete of the Year award six times. She was beloved by fans for her charisma, her humor, and her undeniable greatness.

Fighting to the End

In 1953, at the peak of her golf career, Babe was diagnosed with colon cancer. Doctors performed emergency surgery, removing a cancerous tumor and performing a colostomy. The prognosis was grim.

Most people would have retired. But Babe wasn't most people.

Just 14 weeks after major surgery, she returned to competitive golf. And in 1954, she won the U.S. Women's Open‚Äîby 12 strokes. It was one of the greatest comebacks in sports history.

"They told me I might never play again," she said afterward. "I told them to watch me."

But the cancer had spread. Over the next two years, Babe underwent more surgeries and treatments. She continued playing golf as long as she could, refusing to let the disease define her.

On September 27, 1956, Babe Didrikson Zaharias died at age 45. Thousands attended her funeral. President Eisenhower sent condolences. The sports world mourned.

But her legacy lived on. She had proven that women could be powerful, competitive, and dominant in sports. She had shattered every limitation society tried to place on her. She had shown the world what greatness looked like.
