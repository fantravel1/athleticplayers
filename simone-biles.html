Against All Odds

Before Simone Biles became the most decorated gymnast in history, before she redefined what's possible in her sport, she was a little girl in foster care, searching for stability in a world that had already shown her too much chaos.

Born on March 14, 1997, in Columbus, Ohio, Simone's earliest years were marked by instability. Her biological mother struggled with drug and alcohol addiction, unable to care for Simone and her three siblings. By age three, Simone and her sister Adria were placed in foster care, bouncing between homes, never knowing if they'd have a place to sleep or food to eat.

"I remember being hungry, scared," Simone would later recall. "But I also remember thinking this couldn't be all there was. There had to be something more."

A Grandfather's Love

In 2003, when Simone was six years old, her maternal grandfather, Ron Biles, and his wife Nellie made a life-changing decision: they would adopt Simone and Adria, giving them the stable home they desperately needed. Ron and Nellie became Mom and Dad, providing not just a house, but a home filled with love, structure, and endless support.

It was Ron and Nellie who noticed Simone's natural athleticism. During a daycare field trip to Bannon's Gymnastix in Houston, six-year-old Simone couldn't stop imitating the gymnasts she saw training. The coaches noticed her raw talent immediately‚Äîher power, her fearlessness, her ability to pick up skills that took other kids months to learn.

"From the moment I stepped into that gym, I knew I was home," Simone says. "Not just because I loved gymnastics, but because I finally had a place where I belonged, where I could be myself, where hard work meant something."

I'm not the next Usain Bolt or Michael Phelps. I'm the first Simone Biles.

Rising to Greatness

Simone's ascent in gymnastics was meteoric. By age 16, she won her first all-around title at the 2013 World Championships. By 2016, she was the face of USA Gymnastics, heading to the Rio Olympics as the favorite in every event.

Rio was a revelation. Simone won four gold medals and one bronze, captivating the world not just with her gravity-defying skills‚Äîthe triple-double, the double-double, elements so difficult they seemed to violate the laws of physics‚Äîbut with her infectious joy and unshakeable confidence.

She wasn't just winning. She was dominating in a way the sport had never seen. Her margins of victory weren't measured in tenths of points but in full points. While other gymnasts performed skills, Simone performed impossibilities.

The Darkness Revealed

But behind the medals and the smiles, Simone was carrying a secret pain that hundreds of her teammates shared. In 2016, as she stood atop Olympic podiums, she was also a survivor of sexual abuse by Larry Nassar, the former USA Gymnastics team doctor who abused more than 265 young athletes under the guise of medical treatment.

In 2018, Simone broke her silence. In a heart-wrenching statement on Twitter, she revealed that she too was among Nassar's victims. "For too long I've asked myself, 'Was I too naive? Was it my fault?' I now know the answers to those questions. No. No, it was not my fault. No, I will not and should not carry the guilt that belongs to Larry Nassar, USAG, and others."

Speaking out required immense courage. Simone became an advocate not just for herself but for all survivors, demanding accountability from the institutions that failed to protect young athletes. She testified before Congress, her voice shaking but unwavering as she detailed the failures of USA Gymnastics, the FBI, and the Olympic Committee.

"These are the moments that define us," she said. "Not the medals we win, but how we stand up when everything is trying to knock us down."

I am more than my accomplishments and gymnastics which I never truly realized before.

Tokyo: A Different Kind of Courage

The Tokyo Olympics, delayed to 2021 by the COVID-19 pandemic, were supposed to be Simone's coronation. At 24, she was returning to cement her legacy as the greatest gymnast ever. The pressure was immense‚Äîthe weight of a nation's expectations, the trauma of Nassar's abuse, the isolation of training during a pandemic.

Then, in the middle of the team final, something went wrong. Simone lost her spatial awareness mid-air‚Äîa terrifying phenomenon gymnasts call "the twisties." She couldn't tell up from down, couldn't feel where her body was in space. Landing even the simplest skills became life-threateningly dangerous.

And so, Simone made the most courageous decision of her career: she withdrew. She stepped back from the competition, choosing her mental health and physical safety over medals and glory.

The backlash was swift. Critics called her a quitter, questioned her toughness, said she let down her team and her country. But Simone stood firm. "I'm not having fun," she explained. "This Olympic Games, I wanted it to be for myself, but I came here and I felt like I was still doing it for other people. It just hurts my heart that doing what I love has been kind of taken away from me to please other people."

What followed was a global conversation about mental health in sports, about the pressure we place on our athletes, about the humanity behind the medals. Simone's withdrawal became a watershed moment, giving permission to athletes everywhere to prioritize their wellbeing.

Five days later, after working through the twisties in practice, Simone returned to compete on balance beam. She modified her routine to stay safe but still delivered a breathtaking performance, earning a bronze medal. The reception wasn't the thunderous applause for gold‚Äîit was something deeper: respect, admiration, love.

The Comeback

After Tokyo, many thought Simone was done with competitive gymnastics. She'd earned that right. But in 2023, at age 26, she shocked the world by announcing her return. Not because she had something to prove to anyone else, but because she loved the sport and wanted to do it on her own terms.

At the 2023 World Championships in Antwerp, Simone reminded everyone why she's called the GOAT. She won four more gold medals, bringing her World Championship total to 23 golds and 30 medals overall‚Äîthe most decorated gymnast in World Championships history.

She debuted yet another skill‚Äîthe Biles II on vault, a Yurchenko double pike so difficult that no other woman has even attempted it in competition. When she performs, the arena holds its breath. When she lands, the roar is deafening.
