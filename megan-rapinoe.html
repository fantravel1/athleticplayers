Taking a Knee: 2016

In 2016, NFL quarterback Colin Kaepernick began kneeling during the national anthem to protest police brutality and racial injustice. He was vilified, called un-American, and ultimately blackballed from the NFL.

Megan was one of the first white athletes to join him.

During a US Women's National Team match in September 2016, Megan kneeled during the anthem. She was the first high-profile white athlete and the first woman to do so.

"Being a gay American, I know what it means to look at the flag and not have it protect all of your liberties," she explained. "I have great pride in my country and our ideals, but I know there are people who don't have that experience. We need to have a reckoning."

The backlash was swift. US Soccer changed its rules, requiring players to stand during the anthem. Megan complied‚Äînot because she agreed, but because she chose her battles strategically. She'd find other ways to fight. And she did.

The Equal Pay Battle

Here's the thing about the US Women's National Team: they've won four World Cups and four Olympic gold medals. They're the most successful soccer team in American history‚Äîmen's or women's. They generate more revenue than the men's team. They have higher TV ratings. They sell more jerseys.

And yet, for years, they were paid a fraction of what the men's team made.

In 2016, five USWNT players filed an Equal Employment Opportunity Commission complaint against US Soccer for wage discrimination. The response from US Soccer? Women's soccer is a different job that requires a lower level of skill.

Let that sink in. The federation representing the most dominant team in the sport claimed that women's soccer required "less skill" than men's.

Sue Bird: Love Is a Battlefield
