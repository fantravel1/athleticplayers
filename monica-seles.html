The Aftermath: PTSD and Lost Years

The Trial That Added Insult to Injury

Monica expected justice. A man had stabbed her on a tennis court in front of thousands of witnesses and cameras. The evidence was indisputable. Surely he would go to prison.

But German courts ruled that Parche was a "mentally ill" Graf fan who acted out of "excessive zeal." He received a two-year suspended sentence and probation. He never spent a single day in jail.

The reasoning? The judge said Parche was "psychologically abnormal" but not legally insane. And because Monica had recovered physically from the wound, the attack was classified as "causing bodily harm" rather than attempted murder.

Monica was devastated. "The man who tried to kill me walked free," she later said. "The justice system failed me completely. That hurt almost as much as the stabbing."

The Fear That Wouldn't Leave

Monica tried to return to tennis. She attempted comebacks in 1994, but each time she stepped onto a court, the fear overwhelmed her. She would scan the crowd obsessively, looking for threats. She couldn't concentrate on the match. Every sudden movement in the stands made her flinch.

She suffered from severe PTSD‚Äîpost-traumatic stress disorder. She had nightmares about being attacked. She developed anxiety and panic attacks. She couldn't eat. She gained weight from stress and inactivity, which the media cruelly mocked.

The Adversity Beyond the Stabbing

Losing Her Father

In 1998, Monica's father Karolj‚Äîher first coach, her biggest supporter, the person who had dedicated his life to her tennis career‚Äîdied of stomach cancer. Monica was 24 years old, still trying to rebuild her career after the attack.

Karolj's death devastated Monica. He had been more than her coach‚Äîhe had been her anchor. After his death, Monica struggled with depression. She gained more weight. She questioned whether she wanted to continue playing tennis without him there to support her.

"When my dad died, I lost my biggest cheerleader," Monica said. "Tennis was always something we shared. Without him, it felt empty."

Monica continued playing for five more years, but the joy was gone. She retired in 2003, saying she had lost her passion for the game.

The Weight Struggle and Media Cruelty

After the attack and during her battle with PTSD and depression, Monica gained weight. She went from a fit 135 pounds to over 170 pounds at her heaviest. The weight gain was a direct result of trauma, anxiety, and depression‚Äîbut the media didn't care.

Tabloids ran cruel headlines about her weight. Commentators mocked her appearance. Fans made brutal comments. She was called "fat," "lazy," "undisciplined." Some suggested she was using the attack as an excuse and had just gotten out of shape.

The cruelty made everything worse. Monica developed an eating disorder‚Äîbinge eating followed by extreme guilt. She tried crash diets, which only made the problem worse. She became deeply insecure about her body.

"I was already dealing with PTSD and depression," Monica said. "And then the world decided to attack me for gaining weight‚Äîweight I gained because someone stabbed me and I was terrified to leave my house. It was like being attacked all over again."

The media's treatment of Monica Seles during this period was one of the cruelest examples of how female athletes are judged not just on their performance but on their appearance.

Monica Seles won 9 Grand Slam titles‚Äîan incredible achievement. But everyone who knows tennis asks the same question: What if she hadn't been attacked?

When Monica was stabbed, she was 19 years old and had already won 9 Grand Slams. At the same age, Serena Williams had won 3. Steffi Graf had won 7. Martina Navratilova had won 1.

Monica was on pace to potentially win 25-30 Grand Slam titles. She was dominating the sport like few players ever had. And then a man with a knife took it all away.

The "what if" haunts Monica's legacy. She's in the Hall of Fame, but she's remembered more for what could have been than for what she achieved. That's the cruelest part of her story‚Äîher greatest accomplishments are overshadowed by what was stolen from her.

Life After Tennis: Advocacy and Healing

Becoming a Voice for Mental Health

After retiring, Monica became an advocate for mental health awareness‚Äîparticularly PTSD. She spoke openly about her struggles, about the panic attacks and nightmares, about the years of therapy it took to feel safe again.

"For so long, I was ashamed," Monica said. "I thought I should just be tough, just get over it. But PTSD doesn't work that way. It's not weakness‚Äîit's a normal response to trauma."

Monica's openness helped destigmatize mental health issues in sports. She showed that even elite athletes‚Äîpeople who seem invincible‚Äîcan suffer from psychological trauma and need help.

She became particularly vocal about athlete safety and security at sporting events. After her attack, tennis tournaments increased security dramatically. But Monica argued that more needed to be done across all sports to protect athletes from deranged fans.

Writing Her Story

In 2009, Monica published her memoir "Getting a Grip: On My Body, My Mind, My Self." The book detailed her struggles with PTSD, depression, eating disorders, and body image issues. It was brutally honest‚Äîand it helped millions of people who faced similar struggles.

The book became a bestseller. Readers praised Monica's vulnerability and courage. Many said the book helped them understand their own trauma and seek help.

"Writing the book was terrifying," Monica said. "But staying silent was more terrifying. I wanted people to know they're not alone."

Finding Love and Peace

In 2014, Monica married businessman Tom Golisano. It was her first marriage‚Äîshe was 40 years old. For years, Monica had struggled with relationships, with trust, with letting people close to her after the attack.

"The stabbing made me afraid of people," Monica admitted. "I built walls around myself. Finding someone I could trust enough to marry took a long time."

Monica now lives a quieter life away from the spotlight. She does occasional tennis commentary and appearances, but mostly focuses on her personal life and advocacy work.

She's spoken about finally finding peace‚Äînot with what happened to her, but with who she is despite what happened.
