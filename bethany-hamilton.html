Halloween Morning, 2003

October 31, 2003. A perfect morning for surfing off Tunnels Beach on Kauai's north shore. The water was crystal clear, the waves were gentle, and 13-year-old Bethany Hamilton was lying on her surfboard, her left arm dangling in the water, talking to her best friend Alana about their plans for the day.

Then, in an instant, everything changed.

A 14-foot tiger shark attacked from below, its jaws closing around Bethany's left arm and a massive chunk of her surfboard. The entire attack lasted less than 15 seconds. When it was over, Bethany's left arm was gone‚Äîsevered just below the shoulder.

There was no pain at first, just shock. The water around her turned red. Her friends screamed. Alana's father, Holt, a lifeguard and first responder, immediately took control. He fashioned a tourniquet from a surf leash and got Bethany to shore.

"I just remember looking at the water and seeing it all red," Bethany later recalled. "I thought, 'That's so weird.' And then I looked at my arm and there was nothing there."

By the time they reached the beach, Bethany had lost over 60% of her blood. She was going into hypovolemic shock. The ambulance ride to the hospital took 30 minutes‚Äî30 minutes where her life hung in the balance.

When she arrived at Wilcox Memorial Hospital, doctors gave her a less than 10% chance of survival. Her father, Tom, was already there‚Äîcoincidentally scheduled for knee surgery that morning. When he heard his daughter was coming in, he gave up his operating room for her.

After seven hours of surgery, Bethany stabilized. She'd survive. But her left arm‚Äîand seemingly her surfing career‚Äîwere gone forever.

I remember thinking, 'Well, that's weird. What am I going to do about this?' I wasn't thinking about surfing. I was just thinking about my arm. It's funny the things that go through your mind.

‚Äî Bethany Hamilton

The Girl Who Lived in the Ocean

Bethany wasn't just any surfer. Born on February 8, 1990, in Lihue, Hawaii, she had literally grown up in the ocean. Her parents, Tom and Cheri, were both surfers who had moved to Kauai for the waves. Bethany started surfing at age 5 and was winning competitions by age 8.

By 13, she was already one of the top young surfers in Hawaii, sponsored by Rip Curl, with a promising professional career ahead of her. Surfing wasn't just what she did‚Äîit was who she was.

And now, lying in a hospital bed with one arm, that identity seemed impossible.

But Bethany had something else: unshakable faith. Raised in a Christian home, she believed God had a plan‚Äîeven if she couldn't see it yet. While she was devastated, she wasn't defeated.

"I asked 'Why?' a lot," she said. "But I never asked, 'Why me?' Because why not me? Why should I be spared when other people go through terrible things every day?"

26 Days: The Return

Three weeks after the attack, Bethany's family took her to Thailand for a mission trip‚Äîsomething they'd planned before Halloween. Bethany wanted to go. She wanted to help people affected by poverty, to get perspective on her own situation.

But what she really wanted was to surf again.

On November 26, 2003‚Äîjust 26 days after losing her arm‚ÄîBethany paddled back into the ocean at the same beach where the attack happened. Tunnels Beach. The same break. The same water.

Her family and friends gathered on the beach, terrified and amazed. Bethany paddled out with one arm, the stump of her left arm visible where it ended at her shoulder. She waited for a wave.

And then she caught one.

Popping up with one arm was nearly impossible. Everything about surfing is designed for two arms‚Äîpaddling, balancing, popping up, turning. Bethany had to relearn it all. That first day, she fell. A lot. But she got up. She tried again. And again. And again.

"It was frustrating at first," she said. "Everything I'd learned how to do, I had to relearn with one arm. But I wasn't going to let that stop me."

Within months, she was competing again. Within two years, she won her first national title.

I don't need easy, I just need possible. And with God, all things are possible. I truly believed that. So I got back in the water.

Relearning Everything

Surfing with one arm requires completely different technique. Bethany had to:

Paddle differently. With only a right arm, she had to paddle in a figure-eight motion to keep the board straight. It was exhausting and inefficient, but it worked.

Pop up differently. The traditional "pop-up" involves both hands pushing off the board. Bethany had to modify it, using her right hand and her legs to spring to her feet in one fluid motion.

Balance differently. Without a left arm for counterbalance, she had to engage her core and use her legs more. Every turn, every cutback required total body awareness.

Duck dive differently. To get through waves when paddling out, surfers "duck dive"‚Äîpushing the board underwater with both arms. Bethany had to develop a technique using just one arm and her shoulder.

Her board shaper designed a custom board with a handle for her to grip with her teeth during duck dives. She tried it once and hated it. "I'm not going to surf with something in my mouth," she said. She figured it out without help.

Two years after the attack, in 2005, Bethany won the NSSA National Championships. She'd beaten able-bodied surfers. Not in an adaptive category‚Äîin the regular competition.

She proved it was possible. And she was just getting started.

Soul Surfer: Sharing the Story

In 2004, just one year after the attack, Bethany published her autobiography, "Soul Surfer: A True Story of Faith, Family, and Fighting to Get Back on the Board."

The book became a #1 New York Times bestseller. Millions of people‚Äîmany who had never heard of surfing‚Äîread about this teenage girl who refused to let a shark attack define her. Parents gave it to their kids. Amputees found hope in it. Athletes were inspired by it.

In 2011, the book became a major motion picture starring AnnaSophia Robb as Bethany, with Helen Hunt and Dennis Quaid as her parents. The film grossed over $47 million and introduced Bethany's story to an even wider audience.

But Bethany wasn't just a story. She was still competing, still pushing boundaries, still proving that limitation is a mindset.

Professional Career and Motherhood

Bethany turned professional and competed on the World Surf League's qualifying series. She surfed in some of the most challenging conditions in the world‚ÄîPipeline, Teahupo'o, big wave contests that terrify even able-bodied surfers.

In 2013, she married Adam Dirks, a youth minister. Many wondered if marriage would slow her down, if she'd retire from competitive surfing.

Instead, she had four children and kept surfing.

She surfed while pregnant. She surfed months after giving birth. She brought her kids to competitions. She showed the world that motherhood and athletic excellence aren't mutually exclusive‚Äîespecially when you've already proven that losing an arm isn't a reason to quit.

"People ask me all the time, 'How do you do it with one arm and kids?'" Bethany said. "And I always think, 'How do people do it with two arms and kids?' It's hard for everyone. You just figure it out."

Courage doesn't mean you don't get afraid. Courage means you don't let fear stop you. I was terrified to get back in the water. But I did it anyway. That's what courage is.

‚Äî Bethany Hamilton

Beyond Surfing: The Advocate

Bethany's impact extends far beyond surfing. She became a powerful advocate for people with disabilities, particularly limb differences. She visits hospitals, talks to amputees, and shows them that life doesn't end when you lose a limb‚Äîit just changes.

She founded Friends of Bethany, a foundation that supports shark attack survivors and traumatic amputees. She's spoken to millions through her books, documentaries, and motivational speaking.

She's partnered with prosthetic companies to help provide limbs to amputees who can't afford them. She's used her platform to advocate for better accessibility in sports and everyday life.

And through it all, she's remained humble. She doesn't see herself as special‚Äîjust as someone who refused to give up.

The Shark Question

People always ask Bethany if she's afraid of sharks. If she still goes to Tunnels Beach. If she ever considered not surfing again.

Her answer is always the same: "Fear is a choice."

She still surfs at Tunnels Beach‚Äîthe exact spot where she lost her arm. She surfs in murky water, at dawn, at dusk‚Äîall the times when sharks are most active. She doesn't surf recklessly, but she doesn't let fear control her.

"The ocean is still my happy place," she said. "Sharks live there. That's just a fact. I could get attacked again. But I could also get hit by a car or struck by lightning. You can't live your life in fear of what might happen."

She's never blamed the shark. "It was just doing what sharks do," she says. "I don't have any anger toward it. I'm actually grateful for what happened, because it gave me a platform to help people."

I wouldn't change what happened to me because then I wouldn't have this chance to help people. I've met so many people who have been through tough times, and if my story gives them hope, then it was all worth it.

‚Äî Bethany Hamilton

The Legacy of Courage

Bethany Hamilton is now in her 30s. She's a wife, a mother of four, a professional surfer, a bestselling author, and one of the most recognizable names in adaptive sports‚Äîeven though she's never competed in an adaptive category.

She proved that you don't need two arms to be a champion. You just need courage, determination, and the refusal to accept limitations that others place on you.

Her story has inspired countless people facing their own "shark attacks"‚Äîcancer, accidents, disabilities, trauma. She shows them that survival isn't enough. You can thrive. You can compete. You can win.

From that terrifying Halloween morning in 2003 to today, Bethany has lived by one simple principle: Don't let fear win. Get back in the water. Chase the waves. Live your life.

Because if a 13-year-old girl can lose her arm to a shark and become a professional surfer anyway, what's stopping you?
