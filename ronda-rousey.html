The Day Everything Changed

Ronda Rousey was 8 years old when she came home to find paramedics carrying her father Ron out of the house. He'd broken his back in a sledding accident years earlier, endured dozens of surgeries, and lived in constant pain. That day in 1995, he'd decided he couldn't take it anymore.

Ron Rousey took his own life. And Ronda's world shattered.

"I was the one who found him," Ronda later revealed. "I saw what happened. I can't forget it. I never will."

The trauma would define her childhood. She stopped speaking for months. Her mother AnnMaria‚Äîherself a world judo champion‚Äîdidn't know how to help her daughter process the grief. The family struggled financially. They moved constantly. At times, they were homeless, living in their car.

Ronda turned inward. She became angry, withdrawn, violent. She got into fights at school. She was suicidal herself at times. "I thought about it a lot," she said. "I thought about just ending it. Being with my dad."

What saved her was judo. The mat was the only place where her rage had purpose.

For a really long time, I thought I was going to follow in my dad's footsteps. It took judo to save me. It gave me a reason to keep fighting‚Äîliterally.

Olympic Bronze and Broken Dreams

Ronda started judo training seriously at age 11. Her mother AnnMaria had been the first American woman to win a World Judo Championship in 1984. Judo was in Ronda's blood.

She was good. Really good. By her teens, she was competing internationally. At 17, she became the youngest judoka to qualify for the 2004 Athens Olympics. She didn't medal, but she learned.

Four years later, at the 2008 Beijing Olympics, Ronda won bronze in the 70kg division. She became the first American woman to medal in Olympic judo in 12 years. She was 21 years old, a world-class athlete with her whole career ahead of her.

Then the money ran out.

Olympic judo doesn't pay. Ronda returned to the U.S. broke, living in her car again, working three jobs‚Äîbartending, waitressing, training fighters. She was an Olympic medalist sleeping in a Honda Accord. The American dream had failed her.

"I thought about quitting," she said. "I thought, 'What's the point? I'm an Olympic medalist and I'm homeless.'"

The UFC Gamble

In 2010, Ronda made a decision that would change combat sports forever: she transitioned to mixed martial arts (MMA).

At the time, UFC President Dana White had famously said women would "never" fight in the UFC. Women's MMA was dismissed as unmarketable, boring, not violent enough. The men's sport was thriving; why risk it with women?

Ronda didn't care what Dana White thought. She started fighting in smaller promotions‚ÄîStrikeforce, King of the Cage. She was dominant. Her signature move was the armbar‚Äîa judo submission that she executed with such precision and speed that most fights ended in under a minute.

She went 6-0 in Strikeforce, finishing every opponent. She became Strikeforce Women's Bantamweight Champion. And finally, Dana White couldn't ignore her anymore.

In 2012, the UFC announced they were creating a women's division. Ronda Rousey would be the first woman to fight in the Octagon.

"They said women would never be in the UFC," Ronda said. "I made them eat those words."

Rowdy Ronda: The Champion

From 2013 to 2015, Ronda Rousey was unstoppable. She became the first UFC Women's Bantamweight Champion and defended her title six times. Her average fight time? Less than one minute.

She finished fights so quickly that fans complained they didn't get their money's worth from pay-per-view. Her armbar was poetry in violence‚Äîgrab the arm, twist, snap. Tap out or break. Fighters feared her.

But Ronda was more than just a fighter. She became a crossover star. She appeared on magazine covers, talk shows, movies. She hosted Saturday Night Live. She was in Fast & Furious films. Sports Illustrated named her the most dominant athlete alive‚Äîahead of LeBron James, Serena Williams, everyone.

She proved Dana White spectacularly wrong. Women's MMA became the UFC's biggest draw. Ronda's fights sold out arenas. Her pay-per-view numbers rivaled the men. She was the highest-paid fighter in the UFC‚Äîmale or female.

The Fall: Holly Holm

November 15, 2015. UFC 193 in Melbourne, Australia. Ronda faced Holly Holm, a former boxing champion. Ronda was undefeated. Invincible. The betting favorite by a mile.

Then, in the second round, Holly landed a head kick. Ronda went down. The referee stopped the fight. Knockout. Ronda's first professional loss.

The world was shocked. But no one was more devastated than Ronda.

"I was sitting in the medical room," she later revealed, "and I was down in the corner, and I was like, 'What am I anymore if I'm not this?' I was literally sitting there thinking about killing myself. In that exact second, I'm like, 'I'm nothing. I'm like, what do I do anymore? No one gives a shit about me anymore without this.'"

She thought about her father. About that day in 1995. About how easy it would be to follow him.

What stopped her? Her then-boyfriend Travis Browne. "I looked up and I saw my man Travis there," she said. "I looked up at him, and I was like, 'I need to have his babies. I need to stay alive.'"

I was really fucking sad after I lost. And I've never been that sad in my life. But you get sad so that you can appreciate being happy. You have to go through the bad to appreciate the good.

‚Äî Ronda Rousey

The Comeback That Wasn't

Ronda tried to come back. At UFC 207 in December 2016, she faced Amanda Nunes for the championship. The fight lasted 48 seconds. Nunes destroyed her. Knockout loss number two.

Ronda never fought in MMA again. She was 29 years old. Her fighting career was over.

"I felt like I had failed everyone," she said. "I felt like I'd let down every woman who believed in me. Every girl who wanted to fight because of me."

She disappeared from public life. She struggled with concussion symptoms, depression, suicidal thoughts. The woman who had been the most famous athlete on Earth couldn't leave her house without having panic attacks.

But slowly, she rebuilt. She transitioned to WWE, where her fighting background and charisma made her a star. She became WWE Raw Women's Champion. She acted in films. She wrote books. She got married and became a mother.
