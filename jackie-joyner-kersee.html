The City That Shaped a Champion

East St. Louis, Illinois, in the 1960s and 70s was one of the most dangerous, impoverished cities in America. Murder rates among the highest in the nation. Unemployment over 25%. Schools crumbling. Entire neighborhoods abandoned. This is where Jacqueline Joyner was born on March 3, 1962.

Her grandmother named her after Jacqueline Kennedy, predicting she would be "the first lady of something." That prediction would prove prophetic‚ÄîJackie would become the first lady of track and field, crowned by Sports Illustrated as the "Greatest Female Athlete of the 20th Century."

But that title came at a cost measured in struggle, sacrifice, and severe asthma attacks that nearly ended her career before it started.

Jackie grew up in a house with no heat, where she and her siblings slept in their winter coats. Her father, Alfred, worked on the railroad and as a construction worker. Her mother, Mary, was a nurse's assistant. They worked constantly but never escaped poverty.

"We were poor, but we were happy," Jackie remembered. "My parents taught us that hard work could overcome anything. They taught us to dream bigger than our circumstances."

But East St. Louis was relentless. Gunshots were the soundtrack to childhood. Drugs and violence claimed friends and neighbors. The community center where Jackie first discovered track and field was her refuge‚Äîthe only safe place in a dangerous world.

I learned early on that if you want something badly enough, you can't let your circumstances dictate your dreams. East St. Louis tried to tell me what I couldn't be. I decided to prove it wrong.

She had her first major attack at age 18, collapsing during high school track practice. The attacks would continue throughout her career, sometimes mid-competition, her lungs seizing as she tried to breathe through events that required superhuman endurance.

In 1980, Jackie earned a basketball scholarship to UCLA. Basketball was her first love‚Äîshe'd been a star in high school. But UCLA's track coach, Bob Kersee, saw something special in her during tryouts.

Four years after the heartbreak of Los Angeles, Jackie arrived in Seoul, South Korea, for the 1988 Olympics as the world record holder and overwhelming favorite. She'd set a new heptathlon world record of 7,215 points earlier that year.

Barcelona 1992: Asthma Attacks and Gold Medals

At the 1992 Barcelona Olympics, Jackie was 30 years old‚Äîancient in track and field. She was defending her Olympic title, but her body was breaking down. The years of training, the asthma, the countless competitions‚Äîthey were taking their toll.

During the heptathlon, she suffered a severe asthma attack. Her chest tightened. She couldn't catch her breath. Medical staff rushed to her side. Many thought she'd have to withdraw.

Jackie refused. "I came here to defend my title," she said. "I'm not quitting."

She used her inhaler, caught her breath, and kept competing. Despite the attack, despite being 30, despite everything‚Äîshe won gold again. She also won bronze in the long jump.

It was her final Olympic heptathlon. She'd won back-to-back golds, never losing an Olympic or World Championship heptathlon after that silver in 1984. Bob's promise had been kept.

The Foundation: Giving Back

Even at the height of her fame, Jackie never forgot where she came from. In 1988, while training for Seoul, she founded the Jackie Joyner-Kersee Foundation to provide youth sports, education, and leadership programs in East St. Louis.

She built community centers, funded after-school programs, created scholarships. She wanted to give kids in East St. Louis the same opportunities she'd had‚Äîa safe place to play, coaches who believed in them, a chance to dream.

"I escaped East St. Louis through sports," she said. "But I never wanted to escape the responsibility of helping the next generation. These are my people. This is my community. I owe it everything."

Her foundation has helped thousands of children over the decades. Many have gone on to college on athletic scholarships. Some have become professional athletes. All have had a chance to see that their zip code doesn't define their future.
