The Dream That Wouldn't Die

September 2, 2013. After 53 hours of non-stop swimming, 64-year-old Diana Nyad stumbled onto the beach in Key West, Florida. She had just swum 110 miles from Cuba to Florida without a shark cage. And she had done something no one had ever done before.

"Never, ever give up," she gasped into the cameras. Those were her first words after completing the impossible.

But this wasn't Diana's first attempt. It was her fifth.

She had first tried the Cuba-to-Florida swim in 1978 at age 28. She failed due to weather and currents. For 30 years, she lived with that failure. And at age 60, she decided to try again.

In 2011, she failed again‚Äîjellyfish stings and asthma attacks forced her to quit. In 2012, she tried three more times. Each attempt ended in failure‚Äîstorms, hypothermia, jellyfish swarms.

Most people would have given up. But Diana Nyad wasn't most people.

"I'm not a quitter," she said. "I'll keep trying until I die or I succeed."

On her fifth attempt, at age 64, she finally made it. She became the first person to swim from Cuba to Florida without a shark cage. And she became a symbol of perseverance, determination, and refusing to accept limitations.

Swimming from Cuba to Florida isn't just difficult‚Äîit's deadly. The 110-mile stretch of ocean is filled with dangers: deadly jellyfish, sharks, unpredictable currents, extreme weather, and hypothermia.

Diana faced all of them. She was stung by jellyfish so many times her face swelled. She swam through the night in complete darkness. She battled hallucinations from exhaustion. She vomited from swallowing seawater.

But she kept swimming. Stroke after stroke. Hour after hour. For 53 hours straight.

When she finally reached Florida, her lips were swollen, her face was blistered, and she could barely stand. But she had done it.
