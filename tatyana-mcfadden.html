Walking on Her Hands

In a cold, understaffed orphanage in St. Petersburg, Russia, a little girl born with spina bifida was learning to move through the world in the only way she could: on her hands.

The orphanage was ill-equipped for a child with disabilities. There was no wheelchair, no physical therapy, no accommodations. The other children walked. Tatyana wanted to move too. So she adapted. She taught herself to walk on her hands.

For the first six years of her life, this was how Tatyana navigated the world‚Äîhand over hand, pulling her paralyzed legs behind her, building incredible upper body strength that would one day make her a champion. She'd race the other children down the hallways, her small hands slapping against the cold floor, her spirit unbreakable despite circumstances that would have crushed most adults.

"I didn't know I was supposed to feel limited," Tatyana says now. "I just knew I wanted to keep up with everyone else, and walking on my hands was how I did it."

In 1994, Deborah McFadden, a commissioner with the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, traveled to Russia on business. During her visit, she toured the orphanage where Tatyana lived. The Fight for Equality

At Atholton High School in Maryland, Tatyana excelled in wheelchair racing. She won medals, broke records, dominated her events. But there was one problem: she wasn't allowed to earn varsity letters or score points for her school because she competed in a wheelchair, not on her feet.

The school claimed allowing wheelchair athletes to letter would give them an "unfair advantage." The irony was cruel‚Äîa girl born with paralysis, who'd spent her earliest years in an orphanage walking on her hands, was being told she had an unfair advantage.

Tatyana and her mother sued. The case went all the way to the Maryland legislature, where 15-year-old Tatyana testified with poise and power, explaining that she just wanted to be treated like any other athlete, to be recognized for her hard work and achievements.

In 2008, Maryland passed "Tatyana's Law," requiring schools to allow students with disabilities to compete for varsity letters and team recognition. The law has since been adopted in other states, opening doors for thousands of disabled student-athletes who came after her.

"I didn't fight for myself," Tatyana explains. "I fought for the girl who comes after me, who deserves to have her hard work recognized, who deserves to know she belongs on that team."

Paralympic Dominance

Tatyana's first Paralympic Games came in 2004 in Athens, when she was just 15 years old. She won silver and bronze medals, announcing herself to the world. But it was just the beginning.

At the 2008 Beijing Paralympics, she won three silver and one bronze. London 2012: four golds. Rio 2016: six medals including four golds. Tokyo 2020 (held in 2021): two more medals, bringing her Paralympic total to 20.
