The Announcement That Shook Tennis

On May 31, 2021, Naomi Osaka‚Äîfour-time Grand Slam champion, world #2, the highest-paid female athlete in the world‚Äîwithdrew from the French Open. The reason? She'd been fined $15,000 for skipping a press conference to protect her mental health. The tennis establishment threatened her with disqualification. So she quit.

In her withdrawal statement posted on social media, Naomi revealed something that shocked millions: "I have suffered long bouts of depression since the US Open in 2018." Three years. Three years of smiling through press conferences while dying inside. Three years of winning Grand Slams while battling demons no one could see.

"The truth is that I have suffered long bouts of depression since the US Open in 2018 and I have had a really hard time coping with that," she wrote. "Anyone that knows me knows I'm introverted, and anyone that has seen me at the tournaments will notice that I'm often wearing headphones as that helps dull my social anxiety."

She was 23 years old. At the peak of her career. And she chose herself over the sport.

2018 US Open: The Win That Broke Her

September 8, 2018, should have been the greatest day of Naomi Osaka's life. At 20 years old, she defeated her childhood idol Serena Williams in the US Open final to win her first Grand Slam title. It should have been pure joy.

Instead, it was a nightmare.

During the match, Serena received multiple code violations from chair umpire Carlos Ramos, including a game penalty. Serena, believing she was being treated unfairly, argued vehemently. The crowd‚Äîlargely pro-Serena‚Äîbooed the officiating. The match descended into chaos.

When Naomi won, the crowd booed through the trophy ceremony. Booed a 20-year-old who had just achieved her lifelong dream. Naomi stood on the court crying, her visor pulled down over her face, trying to hide her tears.

"I'm sorry it had to end like this," Naomi said through sobs during her victory speech. She was apologizing for winning.

Serena tried to comfort her, telling the crowd to stop booing, that Naomi deserved to celebrate. But the damage was done. What should have been Naomi's coronation became about everything but her excellence.

That night marked the beginning of her depression. "I felt like I had to apologize for everything I did," she later revealed. "That feeling never really went away."

The Press Conference Battle

By 2021, Naomi had won four Grand Slam titles. She was ranked #2 in the world. She'd been named the highest-paid female athlete in the world by Forbes, earning over $55 million from prize money and endorsements.

On paper, she had everything. In reality, she was drowning.

Mandatory press conferences were her nightmare. Sitting in front of dozens of reporters, cameras flashing, questions flying‚Äîmany of them insensitive, some outright hostile. For someone with severe social anxiety, it was torture.

"I feel like the press conferences cause me a lot of anxiety," she explained. "Particularly when I haven't played well. I have seen clips of athletes breaking down after a loss in the press room. I find that really disturbing."

Before the 2021 French Open, she announced she wouldn't do press conferences, offering to accept fines instead. The tennis establishment responded with fury. She was fined $15,000. The Grand Slam tournaments issued a joint statement threatening her with disqualification and suspension from future tournaments.

The message was clear: Your mental health doesn't matter. Play and talk, or get out.

So she got out.

Tokyo 2021: Coming Home

Just two months after withdrawing from the French Open, Naomi stood in the Tokyo Olympic Stadium, holding the Olympic torch. In front of her home country, she lit the Olympic cauldron‚Äîone of the highest honors in sports.

It was a moment of vindication. Japan, which had criticized her for years, was now celebrating her as the face of the Olympics. The shy, anxious girl who never felt Japanese enough was literally carrying the flame for her country.

But the pressure was immense. Expectations were crushing. And Naomi, still healing, wasn't ready.

She lost in the third round. It hurt, but she'd learned something more important than winning: it was okay to not be okay. It was okay to step back. It was okay to choose healing over medals.

The Ripple Effect

Naomi's French Open withdrawal didn't just change her life‚Äîit changed sports.

Simone Biles withdrew from events at the Tokyo Olympics weeks later, citing mental health. She directly credited Naomi's courage for giving her permission to do the same.

Michael Phelps, who had battled depression and suicidal thoughts, spoke publicly in support. "We're humans, not robots," he said. "We have feelings. We go through different emotions."

Athletes across every sport began speaking up. The stigma around mental health in athletics, which had been ironclad for generations, began to crack.

Organizations started rethinking mandatory media obligations. The tennis tours created new mental health resources. Conversations changed.

Naomi lost a tournament, but she sparked a revolution.

Finding Balance

Since that watershed moment in 2021, Naomi has stepped away from tennis multiple times. She's taken breaks. She's spoken openly about therapy. In 2023, she became a mother, welcoming a daughter with her partner, rapper Cordae.

Her relationship with tennis is complicated now. She loves the game, but she refuses to sacrifice her sanity for it. Some critics say she's wasting her talent. Others say she's soft, that champions push through.

But Naomi knows what they don't: there's no trophy worth your life.

"I've learned that you can't pour from an empty cup," she said. "For so long, I was trying to be everything to everyone‚Äîthe champion, the activist, the role model, the perfect Japanese ambassador, the perfect Black representative. I forgot to be Naomi."

She's redefining what success looks like. Not just Grand Slams, but peace. Not just rankings, but joy. Not just winning, but living.

The Girl Who Said No

Naomi Osaka's story isn't finished. She's still young, still evolving, still figuring out who she is beyond the baseline.

But her legacy is already cemented. She's the girl who said no. No to the press conference. No to the fines. No to the people telling her to just shut up and play. No to sacrificing her mental health for their entertainment.

She proved that you can be at the top of your sport and still be struggling. That success and suffering aren't mutually exclusive. That walking away isn't weakness‚Äîsometimes it's the bravest thing you can do.
