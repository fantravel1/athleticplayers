The Phone Call That Changed Everything

June 30, 2015. Misty Copeland's phone rang. It was Kevin McKenzie, the artistic director of American Ballet Theatre. "Misty," he said, "we're promoting you to principal dancer."

Misty froze. Principal dancer. The highest rank in ballet. The position reserved for the absolute best. The title that, in ABT's 75-year history, had never been held by a Black woman.

She had dreamed of this moment her entire career. But part of her never believed it would actually happen. Because ballet‚Äîone of the world's most prestigious art forms‚Äîhad always been one of its whitest.

Ballet companies wanted dancers who fit a certain image: pale skin, tiny frames, European features. Black dancers were told they were the "wrong color." That their bodies were "too muscular." That they didn't fit ballet's aesthetic.

Misty Copeland had heard all of it. She had been told she was too short, too curvy, too Black. She had been rejected, overlooked, and underestimated.

And now, she was a principal dancer at one of the most prestigious ballet companies in the world.

When the announcement was made public, the reaction was overwhelming. The New York Times ran a front-page story. President Obama congratulated her. Young Black girls around the world saw her and thought, for the first time, "I can be a ballerina too."

Misty had shattered ballet's highest glass ceiling. And she had done it on her own terms.

"For the first time in my life, I felt like I belonged. Not in spite of being Black, but as I am. Ballet tried to tell me I didn't fit. I proved them wrong."

From the Motel to the Stage

Misty Danielle Copeland was born on September 10, 1982, in Kansas City, Missouri. She was the fourth of six children born to her mother, Sylvia DelaCerna, who had been married multiple times and struggled to provide for her large family.

Misty's childhood was chaotic. The family moved constantly‚ÄîKansas City, then San Pedro, California, then various cities across Southern California. Sometimes they lived in apartments. Sometimes they lived in motels. There were periods when they didn't know where they would sleep at night.

Sylvia worked multiple jobs, but money was always tight. The kids wore hand-me-downs. Meals were often whatever they could afford. Stability was a luxury they couldn't afford.

For Misty, the instability was terrifying. She was shy, anxious, and desperate for something solid to hold onto. She found that solace in performing‚Äîdancing in her living room, putting on shows for her siblings.

At school, she was quiet and reserved. But on the drill team‚Äîa dance squad that performed at football games‚Äîshe came alive. Her coach, Cynthia Bradley, noticed immediately: this kid had something special.

When Misty was 13, Cynthia took her to a ballet class at the local Boys & Girls Club. It was Misty's first ballet class ever.

Most professional ballerinas start training at age 3 or 4. By 13, they've already spent a decade building technique. Misty was a decade behind.

But within months, it was clear: Misty was a prodigy. She absorbed technique faster than anyone had ever seen. She had natural musicality, grace, and an ethereal quality that couldn't be taught.

Her teacher, Cindy Bradley, became her mentor, her mother figure, and eventually, her legal guardian when family turmoil made it impossible for Misty to live at home.

"I started ballet at 13. Everyone said it was too late. I didn't know any better. So I just worked."

"You Have the Wrong Body for Ballet"

As Misty rose through the ranks of professional ballet, she faced a constant, crushing message: your body is wrong.

Ballet has historically demanded a very specific body type: extremely thin, long-limbed, pale-skinned. Misty had curves. She had muscles. She had brown skin. And the ballet world let her know, again and again, that she didn't fit.

Directors told her she was "too short" (she's 5'2"). Casting directors said she was "too curvy." Other dancers whispered that she was "too Black" to play classical roles like Odette in Swan Lake.

For years, Misty struggled with body image. She developed disordered eating patterns, trying desperately to fit the mold ballet demanded. She felt like she was constantly fighting her own body.

"I starved myself," she later admitted. "I hated my body. I looked at other dancers‚Äîthin, white, 'perfect'‚Äîand I thought I would never be good enough."

The racism was sometimes subtle, sometimes overt. She was often the only Black dancer in the company. Costume designers struggled to find tights and shoes in her skin tone. Casting directors passed her over for lead roles, claiming she "didn't look the part."

When she finally performed as Odette in Swan Lake in 2014‚Äîthe first Black woman to do so in ABT's history‚Äîit was revolutionary. Young Black girls saw her and finally had a ballerina who looked like them.

"Ballet tried to make me ashamed of my body. Tried to make me believe that brown skin and curves didn't belong on stage. But I refused to change who I am. And eventually, ballet had to change instead."

Breaking Through, Breaking Down Barriers

Misty's promotion to principal dancer in 2015 was more than a personal achievement. It was a cultural moment that forced the ballet world to confront its racism and exclusivity.

Suddenly, everyone was talking about diversity in ballet. Companies that had been all-white for decades started recruiting dancers of color. Ballet schools began offering scholarships to students from underrepresented communities.

Misty became a symbol‚Äînot just of Black excellence, but of the possibility that ballet could evolve, could become more inclusive, could make room for bodies and faces that had been excluded for centuries.

She also became a mainstream celebrity. Under Armour signed her as a brand ambassador‚Äîthe first time a major athletic brand had partnered with a ballerina. Her Under Armour commercial, featuring her dancing while a voiceover recites all the things she was told she couldn't do, went viral.

She wrote bestselling memoirs. She appeared on magazine covers. She met President Obama. She became a role model not just for aspiring ballerinas, but for anyone who had ever been told they didn't belong.

The Legacy of Belonging

Today, Misty Copeland continues to dance as a principal at American Ballet Theatre. But her impact extends far beyond the stage.

She runs programs to bring ballet to underserved communities. She mentors young dancers of color. She speaks openly about body image, racism, and the need for greater diversity in the arts.

And most importantly, she exists. She performs on the world's greatest stages as a Black woman with curves and muscles and brown skin‚Äîproving, night after night, that ballet doesn't have just one look.

Young Black girls see her and dream. They see that ballet is for them too. They see that they don't have to change who they are to belong.

"I get messages all the time from young dancers," Misty says. "They tell me, 'I saw you and I realized I could do this too.' That's why I keep dancing. That's why I keep fighting. Because representation matters."

Misty Copeland didn't just break ballet's color barrier. She redefined what a ballerina can look like, who can dream of being one, and what excellence means in an art form that desperately needed to evolve.

She came from a motel. And she became a principal. That's the American dream. That's the power of refusing to give up. That's Misty Copeland.

"I didn't fit ballet's mold. So I broke the mold. And now, young girls who look like me can see themselves on stage. That's everything."
